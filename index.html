What beer(s) interest you?

Your essential guide to the styles of beer

Have you ever felt curious about ordering a special beer at your local brew pub but opted out for a Heineken in the last second? Well, your days of being intimidated by esoteric microbrews are over!

This easy to follow guide breaks down the basics of individual beer styles without getting into the chemistry or technical aspects. You don't need to understand it to enjoy it!

Lagers

The most common beers today. Lagers can be light or dark, dry or sweet. Lager was originally developed in Germany in the 16th century (somewhat unintentionally). "Lager" comes from the german word for "storeroom". Lagers require fermentation at a low temperature, which is why they were originally produced in "storerooms".

Bohemian Pilsener

A straw colored ligt-bodied lager fit for everyone. Utilizes Czech noble hopes which provide a slightly floral aroma. It should ideally be served with a head of thick, white foam.

ABV range: 4.1% - 5.1%

German Pilsener

German-style pilsners are very pale and fairly light-bodied lagers, typically dry and quite bitter, utilizing German noble hops.

ABV range: 4.6% - 5.3%

Dortmunder

A golden easy-to-drink lager. Neither too sweet nor too dry. Commonly reffered to as "European-style export lager" this is perhaps the most mass-produced beer of all time.

ABV range: 5.1% - 6.1%

American Lager

American-style lager covers a vast school of beer. Unanimously, they should be pale, crisp, and heavily carbonated. They sometimes contain sugar from corn or rice. "Easy drinkin'" describes it best.

ABV range: 2.8% - 6%

Helles

Originating from Munich but popular all throughout Bavaria the Helles lager is typically a bit sweeter than its pale cousins. It should offer less bitterness too.

ABV range: 4.8% - 5.6%

Vienna Lager

Identified by its signature copper-to-red-brown color provided by Vienna malts, this slightly sweet lager often has subtle roasted notes and a richer malt flavor than most light to medium-bodied lagers.

ABV range: 4.8% - 5.4%

Light Ales

There are a great many different light colored beers belonging to the ale family. Ranging from hoppy American beers to German wheat beer, to the sweet Belgium Abbey styles. This category has something for everyone. Below are some popular key styles to help you navigate this vast beerscape.

Pale Ale

A medium-bodied ale that by today's standard isn't always very "pale". The most common trait of this beer is an earthy or floral bitterness It is often reffered to as English-style pale ale since the beer boom of the late 90's.

ABV range: 4.4% - 5.3%

American Pale Ale

Another hops-forward style which had its big up-swing in late 90's California. It usually boasts lots of american hops, giving it a varying aroma from herbal to tropical. In either case its typically more nuanced than its Brittish sibling from which it is derived.

ABV range: 4.4% - 7%

I.P.A.

India Pale Ale quickly established itself as one of the more popular beer styles of the 21st century. Today it various greatly in color from bright golden to dark amber. Unanimously it contains lots of hops, and has a bitterness to reflect it!

ABV range: 4.5% - 7.5%

Wiezen

A family of German beers all feautring wheat. They range in color from straw to amber and are typically cloudy. They are usually distinguishable by their frothy head and their sweet fruity aroma. Weissebiers often have distinguishable notes of banana, peach, and cloves.

ABV range: 4.8% - 5.6%

Bitter

There are several sub-styles of the Brittish bitter, the most noteworthy being E.S.B. (Extra Special Bitter) which is usually has a deep copper color and, despite what its name would suggest, its not very bitter, but rather well balanced. They are not overwhelmingly fruity and have a fairly low carbonation level.

ABV range: 3% - 5.8%

Dark Ales

Welcome to the dark side! These ales have a broader range of characteristics than they get credit for. Some are sweet and savory, and some are dry and roasty. These beers can sometimes be quite strong, but there are lower ABV ones too! The one thing they do have in common is the utilization of darker malts.

Red Ale

Brewed 2 ways: Irish or American. The Irish style should have more noticeable caramel notes from the malts, whereas the American version focuses more on hops and tend to be fruitier. They also typically have a higher ABV.

ABV range: 4% - 7.9%

Porter

A very dark Brittish ale from 18th century London. It's named after the porters of often drank them. Today they can vary alot in both richness and dryness. Traditionally they should be towards the bitter side. Notes of chocolate and coffee are common.

ABV range: 4.4% - 6.6%

Stout

Today "Stout" is often afilliated with Guiness, though name was originally "Stout Porter", which meant "Strong Porter". Todays definitions are blurry when comparing Stouts and Porters, most commonly though stouts are even darker and often carry stronger roasted notes.

ABV range: 4.1% - 8%

Imperial Stout & Porter

Legend has it that "Imperial" was the name given to the strong beer commisioned by Russian imperials. With todays usage it's safe to assume that the beer is of high ABV and packs heaps of flavor! Should not be served too cold.

ABV range: 7% ~ 15%
